# Passwords and Authentication: Best Practices

Your password is the front door to your digital life. A weak password is an open invitation. Here's how to lock things down properly.

## What Makes a Strong Password?

A strong password is long, unique, and unpredictable. The old advice of mixing uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols is fine, but length matters more.

- **Good**: `correct-horse-battery-staple` (passphrase, 28 characters)
- **Bad**: `P@ssw0rd!` (complex but short and common)

Aim for at least 16 characters. Passphrases — random words strung together — are both strong and memorable.

## Never Reuse Passwords

If one site gets breached (and they all eventually do), attackers will try that password everywhere. Use a unique password for every account.

"But how do I remember 50 different passwords?" You don't. That's what password managers are for.

## Password Managers

A password manager stores all your passwords in an encrypted vault. You only need to remember one master password.

Popular options:
- **Bitwarden** — free and open source
- **1Password** — great for families
- **KeePass** — local, offline storage

Generate random passwords for each site and let the manager handle the rest.

## Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

2FA adds a second layer of security. Even if someone steals your password, they can't log in without the second factor.

Types of 2FA:
- **SMS codes** — better than nothing, but SIM swapping is a real attack
- **Authenticator apps** (Google Authenticator, Authy) — much more secure
- **Hardware keys** (YubiKey) — the gold standard

Enable 2FA on every important account: email, banking, social media.

## Biometrics

Fingerprint readers and face recognition are convenient, but they're not perfect. They're great for everyday convenience, but they shouldn't be your only protection.

Biometrics can't be changed if compromised, so combine them with a strong PIN or password.

## Common Attacks

- **Phishing** — fake login pages steal your credentials
- **Brute force** — automated tools try every possible combination
- **Credential stuffing** — using leaked passwords from other breaches
- **Shoulder surfing** — someone watching you type

Stay alert, use 2FA, and never enter your password on a site you reached through an email link.

> 💡 Tip: Check [haveibeenpwned.com](https://haveibeenpwned.com) to see if your email has appeared in any data breaches. If it has, change that password immediately.
